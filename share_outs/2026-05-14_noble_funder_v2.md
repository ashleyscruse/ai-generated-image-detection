# NOBLE Phase 1: A Domain-Matched Benchmark for AI-Image Detection on Law Enforcement Imagery

**Technical brief, v1.1 release**
**Author:** Ashley Scruse, PhD, Morehouse College
**Prepared:** 2026-05-11 for 2026-05-14 share-out
**Funder:** National Organization of Black Law Enforcement Executives

---

## 1. Motivation

AI-generated imagery has begun appearing in legal proceedings as submitted evidence (e.g., *Mendones v. Cushman*, 2025; the deepfake claim in *USA v. Reffitt*, 2022) and is the subject of proposed Federal Rule of Evidence 707, which addresses admissibility of machine-generated content. Detection tools marketed to forensic users have been evaluated almost exclusively on clean, web-sourced imagery (Corvi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020; the NIST Guardians evaluation, 2025), not on the imagery actually generated by surveillance, body camera, or dashcam systems.

This is a domain-coverage problem. Detection models trained on social-media-like imagery may not generalize to law enforcement capture conditions, which are characterized by JPEG compression artifacts, lower native resolution, motion blur, mixed illumination, and lossy storage pipelines. If existing detectors fail systematically on this domain, current deployment recommendations are unsound for the very evidentiary use cases that prompted Rule 707.

We constructed a benchmark to test this empirically.

## 2. Related work and the gap this benchmark addresses

Prior AI-image detection benchmarks (CNNDetection, Wang et al., 2020; the diffusion-image detection set in Corvi et al., 2023; GenImage, Zhu et al., 2023; the NIST 2025 Guardians evaluation) use general-domain imagery (web photos, ImageNet derivatives) and target a clean-image evaluation regime. Outside the AI-detection literature, UCF Crime (Sultani et al., 2018) is the standard surveillance-domain video benchmark, but it is used for anomaly detection rather than image authentication.

Three properties relevant to forensic deployment are not jointly addressed by the existing landscape: real imagery drawn from law enforcement surveillance sources, capture-condition degradation simulating bodycam and CCTV quality, and paired-prompt cross-architecture synthetic generation. Without these properties combined in a single evaluation, claims about detector performance on web-domain imagery do not transfer to the operational conditions departments and courts actually face. The benchmark presented here is constructed to fill that gap.

## 3. Dataset construction

### 3.1 Real images

Real images are sampled from the UCF Crime Dataset (Sultani et al., 2018), stratified across 14 anomaly categories (Abuse, Arrest, Arson, Assault, Burglary, Explosion, Fighting, Robbery, Shooting, Shoplifting, Stealing, Vandalism, Road Accidents) plus a NormalVideos baseline. Stratification was performed at the per-video level, with approximately 360 frames sampled per category (NormalVideos receives 720 to support a non-incident baseline). Total real images: **7,746**.

This is below the originally scoped 10,000-image real-image target. The current benchmark covers UCF Crime exclusively. A general-domain comparison set (e.g., Open Images V7) is planned for v1.2 to support a domain-gap analysis (Section 7.1).

### 3.2 Synthetic images

Synthetic images were generated by three open-weight diffusion models selected to span the architectural landscape relevant to current and near-future fabricated-evidence threats:

| Model | Architecture | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| FLUX.1-schnell | DiT (transformer) | Current SOTA; represents the architectural frontier detectors are largely untrained on |
| SDXL base 1.0 | UNet, multi-stage | Established baseline used in prior detection benchmarks (Corvi et al., 2023; NIST 2025) |
| Realistic Vision 5.1 | UNet, SD 1.5 fine-tune | Most-downloaded photoreal fine-tune on Civitai; ecologically valid as in-the-wild fabricator tool |

Each model received the same set of 240 prompts (paired-prompt design), distributed across four content categories: surveillance_security (100), evidence_style (60), bodycam_style (50), documents (30). Each prompt was rendered with 15 random-seed variations, with seeds matched across generators to enable paired statistical comparison. Total synthetic images per generator: 3,600. Aggregate synthetic: **10,800**.

Generation was performed on TACC Vista GH200 nodes. Resolution: 1024x1024 for FLUX and SDXL; 512x512 (native) for Realistic Vision.

### 3.3 Capture-condition degradation

Each image was rendered at three quality levels, applied via the augmentation pipeline in `src/augmentation/degrade.py`:

| Level | Parameters | Target capture condition |
|---|---|---|
| Clean | None | High-quality digital photo |
| Moderate | JPEG Q50; Gaussian blur sigma=1.0; contrast factor 0.8 | Surveillance camera in good conditions |
| Heavy | JPEG Q30; downscale 0.5x; Gaussian noise sigma=25; Gaussian blur sigma=2.0 | Older bodycam or low-bitrate CCTV |

Degradation parameters were selected to span the visual-quality range observed in real bodycam and surveillance footage (verified informally by visual inspection against publicly available department release footage). A formal validation study comparing these synthetic degradations against real bodycam quality distributions is left for future work.

Total instances after degradation: **74,184** (18,546 raw + 55,638 processed).

## 4. Evaluation methodology

### 4.1 Detector under test

The benchmark was evaluated against `umm-maybe/AI-image-detector` (HuggingFace), a ViT-based binary classifier outputting "artificial" or "human" labels with associated confidence scores. This detector was selected as the open-source representative of the off-the-shelf detection category. Inference was performed locally on TACC Vista, batch size 16, fp16 precision.

The benchmark's intended use is to support evaluation of additional detectors. Commercial detection APIs (Hive Moderation, AI or Not) are planned for v1.2 (Section 7.1).

### 4.2 Metrics

For each (degradation level) cell, we compute: accuracy, precision (for the synthetic class), recall, F1, and AUC-ROC. AUC-ROC is computed using the detector's continuous score for the synthetic class as the discriminative variable. The convention is that AUC = 0.5 corresponds to a random classifier; AUC < 0.5 indicates the score is anti-correlated with the true label.

Ground truth labels are derived deterministically from data provenance: any image sampled from UCF Crime is labeled real; any image produced by a diffusion model in this pipeline is labeled synthetic.

## 5. Results

Detector performance, aggregated across categories and generators within each degradation level:

| Quality | Accuracy | Precision | Recall | F1 | AUC-ROC |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Clean | 0.409 | 0.485 | 0.256 | 0.335 | 0.367 |
| Moderate | 0.372 | 0.343 | 0.085 | 0.136 | 0.189 |
| Heavy | 0.155 | 0.202 | 0.153 | 0.174 | 0.086 |

n = 18,546 per row (7,746 real + 10,800 synthetic).

### 5.1 Observations

**The detector underperforms random chance at every degradation level on this domain.** AUC values across all three levels lie below 0.5. The clean-image AUC of 0.367 indicates that even before any capture-condition degradation is applied, the detector's confidence scores are anti-correlated with the true synthetic/real label on law enforcement imagery.

**Performance degrades monotonically with image quality.** Both accuracy and AUC fall as degradation increases. AUC drops from 0.367 (clean) to 0.086 (heavy), a roughly 4x reduction.

**Recall on the synthetic class is uniformly low.** The detector misses the majority of AI-generated images (recall 0.085–0.256 depending on level), which corresponds to a high false-negative rate. From a forensic standpoint, this is the more consequential failure mode: synthetic evidence being passed as authentic.

Figures (in `results/figures/` and at the dataset HuggingFace page) include accuracy and F1 by degradation level, plus per-level metric heatmaps.

## 6. Limitations

1. **Single detector evaluated.** Conclusions are specific to `umm-maybe/AI-image-detector`. Generalization to commercial detection products requires v1.2 evaluation against Hive and AI-or-Not.
2. **Single real-image domain.** All real images are drawn from UCF Crime. We do not yet have a non-surveillance "general domain" comparison set, which means we cannot decompose failure into a detection failure versus a surveillance-specific domain-shift failure. Planned for v1.2.
3. **Degradation parameters are hand-tuned approximations.** No formal validation against the actual statistical distribution of bodycam or CCTV footage was performed in this release.
4. **Prompt set is human-authored, not stratified against real LE imagery distribution.** Prompts target plausible LE scenes but were not constructed from a sampling frame of actual incident types.
5. **No human-fabricated images in the benchmark.** All synthetic images are model-generated. Adversarial human-edited images (Photoshop composites, frame splicing) are out of scope.

## 7. Next steps

### 7.1 Phase 1 v1.2 (within ~2 weeks)

- Add ~2,000 general-domain real images from Open Images V7 to enable domain-shift decomposition.
- Run Hive Moderation API on a stratified subset of 3,600 images (~$5–55 depending on API credits availability).
- Optionally add AI-or-Not as a second commercial detector.
- Republish dataset as v1.2 with full evaluation across free + commercial detectors.

### 7.2 Phase 2 (Fall 2026, pending funding)

- Train a detection model on the benchmark and demonstrate improvement over off-the-shelf tools.
- Develop a procurement-evaluation document for departments assessing AI-detection vendor claims.
- Submit paper to IEEE Workshop on Information Forensics and Security (WIFS) or comparable venue.

### 7.3 Phase 3+ (longer-term)

- Domain-matched detector available to NOBLE member departments.
- Policy brief supporting Federal Rule 707 development and state-level analogs.
- Periodic benchmark refresh as new generator architectures emerge.
