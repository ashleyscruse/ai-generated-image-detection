## Track 1: Literature Review

You are building the foundation for the Related Work section of our evaluation paper. The research question is:

> **How well do existing AI-generated image detection tools perform on law enforcement content?**

### What to look for

Focus your reading on these areas:

1. **AI-generated image detection methods** — How do current detectors work? What are they trained on? What are their known weaknesses?
2. **Image forensics in law enforcement** — What tools do agencies currently use? How is digital evidence authenticated?
3. **Adversarial robustness / degradation** — How does image quality (compression, blur, noise) affect detection accuracy?
4. **Legal admissibility of digital evidence** — What standards exist (Daubert, Frye)? How are courts handling AI-generated content?

### Starting points

These are known relevant works. Read these first, then follow their citations:

| Paper | Why It Matters |
|-------|---------------|
| Wang et al. (2020) — "CNN-generated images are surprisingly easy to spot...for now" | Foundational detection paper; shows one classifier can generalize across generators |
| Groh et al. (2022) — "Deepfake detection by human crowds, machines, and machine-informed crowds" | Human vs. machine detection accuracy |
| Corvi et al. (2023) — "On the detection of synthetic images generated by diffusion models" | Detection specifically for diffusion model outputs (SDXL, etc.) |
| Mandelli et al. (2022) — "Detecting GAN-generated images by orthogonal training of CNN" | Alternative detection approaches |
| Farid (2022) — "Creating, Using, Misusing, and Detecting Deep Fakes" | Broad survey including legal implications |
| Mendones v. Cushman (case law) | 9 deepfake videos submitted as court evidence, discovered and dismissed |

### Where to search

- [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/) — search terms: "AI generated image detection," "deepfake detection degradation," "synthetic image forensics"
- [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/) — good for finding related papers and citation graphs
- [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/) — search cs.CV (Computer Vision) for latest preprints
- [ACM Digital Library](https://dl.acm.org/) — conference papers from CCS, MM, etc.
- Morehouse library databases — access through the library portal

### How to write each entry

For each paper, write a summary in this format in your research notebook:

```
## [Author] et al. ([Year]) — "[Short Title]"

**Full citation:** [APA format]

**What they did:** [2-3 sentences on methodology]

**What they found:** [2-3 sentences on key results]

**Relevance to our project:** [1-2 sentences on why this matters for our work]

**Limitations / gaps:** [What didn't they address that we might?]
```

## Track 3: Legal & Policy Research

This feeds the policy brief we deliver to NOBLE, which is one of the grant deliverables.

### Questions to answer

1. **How do courts currently authenticate digital evidence?** What standards apply? (Federal Rules of Evidence, Daubert standard, chain of custody)
2. **Have any cases involved AI-generated evidence?** Find cases like Mendones v. Cushman. What happened? What did the court do?
3. **What guidance exists from law enforcement organizations?** Check NOBLE, IACP, DOJ, FBI, NIST for any published guidance on AI-generated content
4. **What are departments actually doing?** Are any agencies using detection tools? Have any issued internal policies?
5. **What are the risks?** Both directions: fake evidence submitted as real, AND real evidence challenged as fake ("the liar's dividend")

### Where to look

- NOBLE publications and conference proceedings
- IACP (International Association of Chiefs of Police) resources
- DOJ/FBI guidance documents
- NIST digital forensics standards
- Legal databases (Google Scholar case law, if you have access to Westlaw/LexisNexis even better)
- News articles about AI evidence in court cases

## Track 4: Image Quality Control

When the team starts generating synthetic images (Part 4), someone needs to review them. This is your job.

### What QC means

For each batch of generated images, you'll review a sample and flag:

- **Off-topic images** — the prompt said "parking garage" but the image is a living room
- **Obvious artifacts** — distorted faces, floating objects, text that doesn't make sense
- **Wrong style** — supposed to look like surveillance footage but looks like a professional photo
- **Duplicates or near-duplicates** — images that are too similar to each other

### How to do it

1. The team will share generated image batches with you (on TACC or via a shared folder)
2. Review a random sample (e.g., 50 out of every 500)
3. Log your findings in your research notebook: image filename, issue found, keep/reject recommendation
4. Report patterns to Dr. Scruse (e.g., "the parking lot prompts are generating indoor scenes half the time")

This track starts later, when image generation is underway. Focus on Tracks 1-3 first.
